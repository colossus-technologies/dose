 41  long_description = '''%s 
 42   
 43  Life is fascinating and deeply intriguing. Despite so, life forms on Earth  
 44  or carbon-based life forms as a group is just one form, one possible sample  
 45  of possibly a whole magnitude of life. Even then, there are many aspects of  
 46  life that cannot be deciphered even by examining current life forms; for  
 47  example, how did chemical reactions organize themselves into biochemical  
 48  pathways? How did life start? How is intelligence formed? 
 49   
 50  To answer such questions, we will have to restart our evolutionary time to  
 51  the very beginning - clearly an impossibly gargantuan task. At the same  
 52  time, studying biological/carbon-based life forms is expensive, time  
 53  consuming and destructive. As a molecular biologist, there is no way I can  
 54  examine the entire genome of even a bacteria in an inanimate state, then  
 55  somehow allow it to continue living as if time had just stopped while I  
 56  am examining it. 
 57   
 58  However, if I can simulate a bacteria or any life form in a computer,  
 59  then I can make a digital copy of the bacterium, pull it apart to study it  
 60  while the original bacterium continues "living" in my virtual world without  
 61  even knowing that it had been duplicated. Many biologists thought of virtual  
 62  life forms as a new way to learn about life itself. Studying of virtual life  
 63  forms is known as Artificial Life and I term "virtual life forms" as  
 64  "digital organisms". There are several advantages in experimenting using  
 65  digital organisms. Firstly, generation time can be much faster compared to  
 66  most biological life. Secondly, it is usually cheaper to examine computer  
 67  simulations than working on actual biological life. Perhaps the most  
 68  important advantage of looking at life from this perspective is that by  
 69  recreating life in a different medium, we are not limited to our own system  
 70  of carbon-based life; hence, studying life as what-it-could-be. 
 71   
 72  Digital Organisms Simulation Environment (DOSE) is essentially a virtual  
 73  world simulator for studying digital organisms. I will argue that digital  
 74  organisms are considered living organisms (Koh and Ling, 2013). Despite so,  
 75  being a molecular biologist by training, I have a hard time mapping  
 76  components of digital organisms into biological life whenever such  
 77  components are too abstract. 
 78   
 79  Hence, I decided to design an artificial life / digital organism simulator  
 80  that bears resemblance to biological life and ecology. These are the  
 81  foundation papers: 
 82   
 83  1. Lim, JZR, Aw, ZQ, Goh, DJW, How, JA, Low, SXZ, Loo, BZL, Ling, MHT. 2010.  
 84  A genetic algorithm framework grounded in biology. The Python Papers Source  
 85  Codes 2: 6.  
 86   
 87  This manuscript describes the implementation of a GA framework that uses  
 88  biological hierarchy - from chromosomes to organisms to population. 
 89   
 90  2. Ling, MHT. 2012. An Artificial Life Simulation Library Based on Genetic  
 91  Algorithm, 3-Character Genetic Code and Biological Hierarchy. The Python  
 92  Papers 7: 5.  
 93   
 94  Genetic algorithm (GA) is inspired by biological evolution of genetic  
 95  organisms by optimizing the genotypic combinations encoded within each  
 96  individual with the help of evolutionary operators, suggesting that GA  
 97  may be a suitable model for studying real-life evolutionary processes.  
 98  This paper describes the design of a Python library for artificial life  
 99  simulation, Digital Organism Simulation Environment (DOSE), based on GA  
100  and biological hierarchy starting from genetic sequence to population.  
101  A 3-character instruction set that does not take any operand is  
102  introduced as genetic code for digital organism. This mimics the 3- 
103  nucleotide codon structure in naturally occurring DNA. In addition,  
104  the context of a 3-dimensional world composing of ecological cells is  
105  introduced to simulate a physical ecosystem. 
106   
107  - Ling, MHT. 2012. Ragaraja 1.0: The Genome Interpreter of Digital  
108  Organism Simulation Environment (DOSE). The Python Papers Source Codes  
109  4: 2.  
110   
111  This manuscript describes the implementation and test of Ragaraja  
112  instruction set version 1.0, which is the core genomic interpreter of  
113  DOSE. 
114   
115  From this foundation, the complete suite of Digital Organisms Simulation  
116  Environment (DOSE) can be build.
